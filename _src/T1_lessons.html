Lesson 1 · Operations on Decimals NA 6NA-Ia-1 / 6NA-Ib-2

Subject MatterThe four fundamental operations on decimals; multi-step word problems
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialsplace-value chart, play money (peso bills/coins), grocery receipts, manila paper
ReferencesMATATAG Math CG Grade 6; Term 1 Budget of Work
Learning Objectives
At the end of the lesson, learners are able to:
(Knowledge) recall the rules for aligning decimal points in addition/subtraction and placing the decimal point in products/quotients;
(Skills) add, subtract, multiply, and divide decimals, and solve multi-step routine and non-routine problems;
(Values) show accuracy and honesty when handling money in real transactions.
Activity — “Tindahan Day.” Groups receive a price list (rice ₱52.75/kg, egg ₱8.50, cooking oil ₱98.25). Each group “buys” items totaling a given budget of ₱200 and computes their change using play money.
Analysis — Guide questions: How did you line up the numbers when adding prices? What happens to the decimal point when you multiply ₱52.75 × 3? When you divide a bill equally among 4 friends, where does the decimal point go?
Abstraction — Formalize the rules:
  • Add/Subtract: align decimal points, then add/subtract as with whole numbers.
  • Multiply: multiply as whole numbers; the product has as many decimal places as the total in the factors.
  • Divide: move the divisor’s decimal to make it a whole number, move the dividend’s the same number of places, then divide.
Worked examples: 12.6 + 4.85 = 17.45; 9.3 − 5.74 = 3.56; 5.2 × 0.6 = 3.12; 4.5 ÷ 0.5 = 9.
Application — Problem: “Aling Rosa bought 2.5 kg of fish at ₱180.00/kg and paid with a ₱500 bill. How much change did she receive?” (Answer: 2.5 × 180 = ₱450; 500 − 450 = ₱50.00.)
Formative Assessment / Assignment
3-item exit pass (one per operation). Assignment: bring one real receipt and verify two computations on it.

Lesson 2 · Adding and Subtracting Fractions and Mixed Numbers NA 6NA-Ic-3

Subject MatterAddition and subtraction of fractions and mixed numbers (like and unlike denominators)
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialsfraction strips/tiles, paper circles, recipe cards
Learning Objectives
(K) explain why fractions need a common denominator before adding/subtracting; (S) add and subtract fractions and mixed numbers, regrouping when needed, and express answers in lowest terms; (V) value teamwork and patience in step-by-step work.
Activity — “Sharing the Bibingka.” Using paper circles cut into halves, thirds, and sixths, groups model 12 + 13 and physically find an equivalent shared piece.
Analysis — Why can’t we just add the numerators and denominators? What single size of piece can represent both halves and thirds? (sixths)
Abstraction — Steps: find the LCD → rename to equivalent fractions → add/subtract numerators → simplify. Example: 12 + 13 = 36 + 26 = 56. Mixed numbers: 314 − 134 = 112 (regroup 1 whole as 44).
Application — “A recipe needs 212 cups of flour and 134 cups of sugar. How many cups of dry ingredients in all?” (Answer: 414 cups.)
Formative Assessment / Assignment
Think-pair-share board work (2 items). Assignment: write one real situation at home that uses fraction addition.

Lesson 3 · Multiplying and Dividing Fractions NA 6NA-Id-4

Subject MatterMultiplication and division of fractions, whole numbers, and mixed numbers
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialsgrid paper, fraction tiles, string/ribbon for measuring
Learning Objectives
(K) state that “of” means multiply and that dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal; (S) multiply and divide fractions/mixed numbers and solve related problems; (V) appreciate precision in measuring and sharing.
Activity — “Half of a Half.” On grid paper, learners shade 12 of a region, then shade 13 of the shaded part and name the result (16).
Analysis — What operation does “13 of 12” suggest? When we cut 3 metres of ribbon into 14-metre pieces, are we multiplying or dividing?
Abstraction — Multiply: multiply numerators, multiply denominators, simplify. Convert mixed numbers to improper first. Divide: multiply by the reciprocal of the divisor. Examples: 23 × 34 = 12;   3 ÷ 14 = 3 × 4 = 12.
Application — “A 312-metre ribbon is cut into 12-metre pieces. How many pieces?” (Answer: 72 ÷ 12 = 7 pieces.)
Formative Assessment / Assignment
Quick board relay (4 items). Assignment: draw a model showing 25 of 10.

Lesson 4 · GCF and LCM NA 6NA-Ie-5

Subject MatterGreatest Common Factor and Least Common Multiple of 2–3 numbers; applications
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialscounters, factor trees on cards, Venn diagram templates
Learning Objectives
(K) distinguish factors from multiples and GCF from LCM; (S) find the GCF and LCM using listing, prime factorization, and continuous division, and apply to problems; (V) demonstrate orderliness and logical thinking.
Activity — “Goody Bags.” Given 24 candies and 36 biscuits, groups find the greatest number of identical goody bags they can make with nothing left over.
Analysis — What number of bags works for BOTH items? Is that the largest such number? How is this different from finding when two blinking lights light up together (LCM)?
Abstraction — Demonstrate three methods (listing, prime factorization, continuous division). Example: 24 = 2³×3, 36 = 2²×3². GCF = 2²×3 = 12; LCM = 2³×3² = 72. GCF → “largest equal groups”; LCM → “next time events coincide / common denominator.”
Application — “Bus A leaves every 12 min, Bus B every 18 min. If both leave at 7:00 a.m., when do they next leave together?” (Answer: LCM(12,18)=36 → 7:36 a.m.)
Formative Assessment / Assignment
Venn-diagram seatwork (GCF & LCM of 16 and 20). Assignment: find GCF/LCM of your & a sibling’s age.

Lesson 5 · Exponents and the GEMDAS Rule NA 6NA-If-6

Subject MatterReading/writing numbers in exponential form; order of operations (GEMDAS)
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialssquare/cube blocks, operation cards, mini-whiteboards
Learning Objectives
(K) identify base and exponent and state the GEMDAS order; (S) write/evaluate numbers in exponential form and simplify expressions using GEMDAS; (V) value following correct procedures and rules.
Activity — “Folding Paper.” Learners fold paper repeatedly and record the number of regions (2, 4, 8, 16…) leading to 2¹, 2², 2³, 2⁴.
Analysis — What does the small raised number tell us? In 2 + 3 × 4, do we add or multiply first — and why does the order change the answer?
Abstraction — Exponential form: bⁿ means b used as a factor n times (e.g. 5³ = 5×5×5 = 125). GEMDAS: Grouping → Exponents → Multiply/Divide (left–right) → Add/Subtract (left–right). Example: 2 + 3 × 4² = 2 + 3 × 16 = 2 + 48 = 50.
Application — Evaluate (6 + 4) ÷ 2 + 3² = 10 ÷ 2 + 9 = 5 + 9 = 14.
Formative Assessment / Assignment
Mini-whiteboard flash (3 expressions). Assignment: create one GEMDAS expression whose answer is 20.

Lesson 6 · Tessellation MG 6MG-Ig-7

Subject MatterIdentifying shapes that tessellate; creating tessellation patterns (Filipino designs)
Time1–2 sessions (50–100 min)
Materialspattern blocks/cutouts (triangles, squares, hexagons, pentagons), colored paper, samples of banig weaving
Learning Objectives
(K) define tessellation and explain why some shapes tessellate and others do not (angles around a point sum to 360°); (S) identify tessellating shapes and create an original tessellation; (V) appreciate geometry in Filipino art and weaving (banig, habi).
Activity — “Tiling the Floor.” Groups try to cover a sheet with no gaps/overlaps using squares, triangles, hexagons, and regular pentagons; they record which ones work.
Analysis — Which shapes left gaps? Look at the angles meeting at a point — what must they add up to? (360°) Why does a regular pentagon (108°) fail?
Abstraction — A tessellation is a repeating pattern of shapes covering a plane with no gaps or overlaps. Regular tessellations exist for the equilateral triangle (60°), square (90°), and regular hexagon (120°) because their angles divide 360° evenly. Connect to Filipino banig mats and habi weaving.
Application — Each learner designs an original tessellation inspired by a Filipino weaving pattern and labels the repeating shape.
Formative Assessment / Assignment
Gallery walk + rubric (correctness, no gaps, creativity). Assignment: photograph/sketch one tessellation found at home or in your barangay.
