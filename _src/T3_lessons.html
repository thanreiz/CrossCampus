Lesson 1 · Properties of Triangles and Quadrilaterals MG 6MG-IIIa-1

Subject MatterClassifying triangles and quadrilaterals by their properties
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialscut-out polygons, geoboards, ruler, protractor
Learning Objectives
(K) name triangles by sides/angles and quadrilaterals by their properties; (S) classify and describe each figure; (V) value precision and order.
Activity — “Shape Sorting.” Groups sort a pile of cut-out shapes into triangles and quadrilaterals, then into sub-groups.
Analysis — What makes a triangle equilateral, isosceles, scalene? Right, acute, obtuse? How is a square different from a rhombus or a rectangle?
Abstraction — Triangles by sides: equilateral, isosceles, scalene; by angles: acute, right, obtuse. The three angles sum to 180°. Quadrilaterals: parallelogram, rectangle, square, rhombus, trapezoid — described by parallel sides, equal sides, and right angles. Angle sum = 360°.
Application — Given two angles of a triangle (50° and 60°), find the third (70°) and classify it (acute).
Formative Assessment / Assignment
Classify-5-figures slip. Assignment: find and name 3 quadrilaterals at home.

Lesson 2 · Perimeter and Area of Plane Figures MG 6MG-IIIb-2

Subject MatterPerimeter and area of triangles, parallelograms, and trapezoids
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialsgrid paper, ruler, cut-out figures, tape measure
Learning Objectives
(K) state the area formulas for triangle, parallelogram, trapezoid; (S) compute perimeter and area; (V) value usefulness of measurement in daily life.
Activity — “Bayong Garden.” Groups measure a triangular and a trapezoidal “garden plot” on grid paper and count squares to estimate area.
Analysis — How does a triangle relate to a rectangle of the same base and height? How can a parallelogram be reshaped into a rectangle?
Abstraction — Perimeter = sum of all sides. Area: triangle = 12×b×h; parallelogram = b×h; trapezoid = 12×(b₁+b₂)×h.
Application — “A triangular sail has base 6 m and height 4 m. Find its area.” (A = 12×6×4 = 12 m².)
Formative Assessment / Assignment
3 area items. Assignment: find the area of a triangular or trapezoidal object at home.

Lesson 3 · Circles — Parts and Circumference MG 6MG-IIIc-3

Subject MatterIdentifying parts of a circle; finding circumference
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialscircular objects (cans, plates), string, ruler, compass
Learning Objectives
(K) name the parts of a circle and state C = πd = 2πr; (S) measure and compute circumference; (V) appreciate circles in nature and design.
Activity — “String Around.” Learners wrap string around cans/plates, measure the distance around and across, and compute the ratio (≈ 3.14).
Analysis — What is the relationship between the distance around (circumference) and across (diameter)? Why is it always about 3.14?
Abstraction — Parts: center, radius, diameter (d = 2r), chord, arc, circumference. π ≈ 3.14. Circumference C = πd = 2πr.
Application — “A bicycle wheel has diameter 60 cm. Find its circumference.” (C = 3.14×60 = 188.4 cm.)
Formative Assessment / Assignment
Label-the-parts diagram + 2 C items. Assignment: measure a round object’s circumference with string.

Lesson 4 · Area of a Circle and Composite Figures MG 6MG-IIId-4

Subject MatterArea of a circle; area of composite figures
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialscircle cut into sectors, grid paper, composite-figure cards
Learning Objectives
(K) state A = πr²; (S) find the area of a circle and of composite figures; (V) value careful, step-by-step problem solving.
Activity — “Pizza Rearrange.” A paper circle cut into sectors is rearranged into a near-parallelogram to motivate A = πr².
Analysis — How does the rearranged shape resemble a parallelogram with base ≈ half the circumference and height = radius? How do we find the area of a figure made of a rectangle plus a half-circle?
Abstraction — Area of a circle A = πr². Composite figures: split into basic shapes, find each area, then add (or subtract for holes).
Application — “Find the area of a circle with radius 7 cm.” (A = 3.14×7×7 = 153.86 cm².)
Formative Assessment / Assignment
2 circle + 1 composite item. Assignment: design a composite logo and compute its area.

Lesson 5 · Data, Pie Graphs, and Measures of Central Tendency DP 6DP-IIIe-5 / 6DP-IIIf-6

Subject MatterConstructing/interpreting pie graphs; computing mean, median, and mode
Time2 sessions (100 min)
Materialssurvey slips, protractor, colored pencils, chart paper
Learning Objectives
(K) read a pie graph and define mean, median, mode; (S) construct a pie graph and compute the three measures; (V) value honesty in collecting and reporting data.
Activity — “Favorite Filipino Food.” Class surveys favorite dishes (adobo, sinigang, etc.) and tallies the results.
Analysis — How can we show parts of a whole at a glance? How many degrees represent each category? Which single number best describes “typical”?
Abstraction — Pie graph: each category’s angle = (its part ÷ total) × 360°. Mean = sum ÷ count; Median = middle value (ordered); Mode = most frequent value.
Application — Scores 7, 8, 8, 9, 10 → mean = 8.4, median = 8, mode = 8. Make a pie graph of a 40-pupil survey.
Formative Assessment / Assignment
Interpret a given pie graph + compute mean/median/mode of 5 numbers. Assignment: survey 5 family members and tally.

Lesson 6 · Simple Experimental Probability DP 6DP-IIIg-7

Subject MatterDescribing and computing the probability of simple events
Time1–2 sessions (50–100 min)
Materialscoins, dice, colored marbles in a bag, spinner
Learning Objectives
(K) use the terms certain, likely, unlikely, impossible and state P(event) = favorable ÷ total; (S) compute simple probabilities; (V) value fairness and reasoning under uncertainty.
Activity — “Lucky Draw.” Groups draw marbles from a bag (3 red, 2 blue) and record outcomes over many trials.
Analysis — Which color came out more often? Was it certain or just likely? How do we put a number on “chance”?
Abstraction — Probability P(E) = number of favorable outcomestotal number of outcomes, a value from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain). Example: a bag of 3 red + 2 blue → P(red) = 35.
Application — “A die is rolled. What is the probability of getting an even number?” (Favorable 2,4,6 → 36 = 12.)
Formative Assessment / Assignment
3 probability items (coin, die, marbles). Assignment: list one certain, one likely, one impossible event at home.
